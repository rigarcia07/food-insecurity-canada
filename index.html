The Quiet Crisis · Canada · 2025–26

Nearly 1 in 4 people in Canada can’t reliably afford food.

In 2025, 24.0% of Canadians — about 9.8 million people, including 2.4 million children — lived in food-insecure households. Here’s what the data actually shows, and what’s driving it.

A fragile turn

The rate dipped for the first time in three years — but the ground is shifting.

2025 brought the first decline after three consecutive record-breaking years. It’s still among the highest levels in two decades of monitoring — and the 2026 outlook is uncertain.

When the safety net strains

Food bank use has doubled in six years.

+5.2%
Even as the insecurity rate dipped, food bank visits rose again over 2024 — to the highest monthly total ever recorded. Demand and the official rate are now telling two different stories.
Root causes

It’s the cost of living, not the cost of food alone.

Housing eats the budget

Low-income households now spend 66% of disposable income on housing — up from 49% in 2021. Little is left for food.

Prices outran wages

Since 2021, shelter is up ~26%, food ~25%, transportation ~20% — while incomes lagged behind.

Benefits below the line

Social assistance is the main income for ~40% of food bank clients, yet sits below the poverty line in nearly every province.

The 2026 tariff wildcard

U.S. tariffs are pushing grocery inflation higher. Advocates warn the modest 2025 improvement may reverse.

What the evidence points to

This is solvable. Canada has set a target.

Food Banks Canada has called for cutting food insecurity in half by 2030. Researchers at the University of Toronto’s PROOF team point to income policy as the most effective lever:

  • →Strengthen the Canada Child Benefit to reach families with the deepest need.
  • →Create a dedicated Groceries and Essentials Benefit for low-income adults.
  • →Modernize Employment Insurance so a job loss isn’t a fall off a cliff.
